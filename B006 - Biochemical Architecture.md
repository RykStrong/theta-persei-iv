# B006 – Laboratory Survey: Biochemical Architecture of TPIV Life

## Summary

This dispatch summarises the preliminary biochemical analysis of collected TPIV biological material, with a focus on the internal structure and functional dynamics of native lifeforms. The findings establish the presence of two novel biological systems — **BLU (Basic Life Units)** and **GFP (Genomic Folded Proteins)** — which together support a modular, distributed mode of biological development and operation unprecedented in Earth biology.

---

## Key Findings

### 1. Absence of Traditional Cells or DNA

No sampled tissue displayed any sign of cellular structures recognisable by Earth standards. There are no nuclei, no cellular membranes, and no visible chromosomes or DNA analogs in the expected configuration. Standard histological staining protocols yielded inconsistent or ambiguous results.

### 2. Presence of BLU Structures

All tissues examined appear to be composed of discrete, integrated macroscopic units — termed **Basic Life Units (BLUs)**. These BLUs resemble Earth cells in function (structural cohesion, metabolic activity, specialisation), but differ in several important ways:

- BLUs are significantly larger than Earth cells
- They do not share a uniform genome; most BLUs only contain subset genetic instructions relevant to their function
- They exhibit modular integration, forming continuous tissue with no rigid boundary layers
- BLU tissue responds to trauma and chemical exposure in ways nearly indistinguishable from multicellular animal tissue

### 3. Presence of GFP Molecules

Within and between BLUs, we identified **Genomic Folded Proteins (GFPs)** — large, complex macromolecules folded into structurally stable forms. These function as the primary vehicle for genetic inheritance and regulatory signalling on TPIV.

- GFPs are biochemically stable in isolation, suggesting that inheritance operates via environmental transfer and assembly
- Each GFP appears to control one or more modular functions or traits, but no single structure carries a full organismal blueprint
- GFP molecules circulate in body fluids, accumulate in certain organs, and appear to be shed into the environment under hormonal or seasonal triggers

### 4. Biochemical Chirality

Chemical assays confirmed the presence of non-homochiral biochemistry. TPIV life does not conform to a universal chirality like Earth's exclusive use of left-handed amino acids and right-handed sugars. Instead, individual organisms contain mixed chirality components, with compartmentalised or selectively paired stereoisomers.

### 5. Known Limitations and Assumptions

- GFP origin and replication processes remain unclear
- Long-term GFP expression and mutation rates are still under investigation
- Several molecular compounds present in both autotroph and heterotroph tissue types remain unclassified by terrestrial standards

---

## Preliminary Conclusions

TPIV biology is modular, scalable, and partially decentralised, relying on a biochemical architecture in which genetic information is both fragmented and mobile. This model offers advantages in adaptive evolution and ecological integration but poses profound challenges for comparative taxonomy.

No single BLU or GFP appears to define the organism alone — only in assemblage does a coherent lifeform emerge. These results support field observations suggesting that organismal identity is dynamic, and potentially transient, with genetic modules entering, exiting, and altering developmental pathways during both growth and reproduction.
