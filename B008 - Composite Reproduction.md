# B008 – Composite Reproduction: Environmental Gene Accumulation and Womb Assembly

## Summary

This dispatch details the biological processes underlying the assembly and birth of new TPIV organisms, focusing on the role of environmental gene accumulation, partifzygote formation, and womb-partifunct orchestration. In contrast to terrestrial sexual reproduction, life on TPIV relies on a distributed genetic environment and modular biological assembly.

Organisms on TPIV reproduce by integrating genetic material (GFP) continuously shed into the environment by surrounding life. These GFP packages are collected by the adult organism and routed to the appropriate internal subsystems, where they are recombined into **partifzygotes** — developmental nucleation structures that give rise to modular subsystems (limbs, sensory organs, digestive tissues, etc.).

All partifzygotes are subsequently transferred to a dedicated internal womb partifunct, where they are evaluated for compatibility, structurally patterned, and assembled into a viable composite offspring.

> **Key note:** Other eGFP can be incorporated into the composite organism in the womb. This is the major feature allowing the omnipresent lack of heterogeneity.

---

## Observed Reproductive Process

### Stage 1 – Environmental GFP Shedding and Accumulation

Adult organisms continuously shed and collect eGFP from ambient sources — via respiration, ingestion, and specialised collection sites in respiratory tracts, glandular pores, and filtering digestive tissues.

- eGFP originates from plant-like fruiting bodies, aerial mites, pollen-like clouds, and aquatic carriers
- Most eGFP is metabolised as nutrient; a subset is retained for recombination

### Stage 2 – Local Recombination & Partifzygote Formation

Each anatomical subsystem hosts its own recombination architecture, where retained eGFP is matched against the internal lineage markers of that subsystem.

- If compatibility thresholds are met, a partifzygote is formed
- Partifzygotes are unique, modular precursors — not gametes

### Stage 3 – Transfer to the Womb Partifunct

The parent's womb partifunct acts as a coordination hub for gestation.

- Compatible partifzygotes are transferred into the womb
- Hormonal signalling and molecular checks are performed to assess viability and compatibility
- Womb tissue polls the parent's other partifuncts via BLU-level signal exchange to establish a composite plan

### Stage 4 – Assembly-Conception

Rather than fertilisation, TPIV organisms undergo **assembly-conception** — the point where partifzygotes initiate cooperative development.

- Partifzygotes organise into integrated anatomical systems
- BLU expression begins within each subsystem
- Structural scaffolding and orientation information are transmitted epigenetically through the womb BLU

### Stage 5 – Birth and Emergence

Once internal systems reach metabolic integrity:
- The composite is released from the womb
- Individual may differ significantly from parents, depending on eGFP flux and selected configurations

## Developmental Implications

- Composite offspring share some taxonomic traits but display significant anatomical variation
- Lineage is established primarily through functional role continuity, not physical resemblance
- Evolutionary adaptation occurs rapidly — composite designs shift in response to eGFP prevalence
