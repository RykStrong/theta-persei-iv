# B010 – GFP Carrier Ecology: Mites, Pollen, and SBLUs

## Summary

This dispatch details the ecological mechanisms by which GFP (Genomic Folded Protein) material is transferred through TPIV's biosphere. Recent drone footage, trap sampling, and tissue analysis confirm the role of a specialised class of carrier organisms — including mites, plankton-like swarms, and pollen-like microbodies — that distribute and sometimes curate GFP in the environment.

These vectors exhibit traits analogous to Earth's reproductive pollen, viral agents, and even symbiotic couriers. Several types are now confirmed as critical to the reproductive ecology of both autotrophs and heterotrophs.

---

## Key Carrier Classes

### 1. GFP Mites ("VT-MIT" Class)
- Arthropod-scale organisms observed across both land and aerial environments
- Fly or crawl between modular organisms, carrying tightly coiled GFP packets
- Often engage in short-lived mutualisms (receiving nutrients from host secretions)
- Some mites appear to specialise in specific GFP "flavours"
- Competing colonies of mites appear to influence which GFP sets dominate reproduction in certain herds

### 2. Pollen-Analogs ("VT-POL" Class)
- Free-floating, structurally simple packets ~2–5 microns
- Passive distribution via wind, evaporation columns, or convection
- Some show strong clustering behaviour around spiracle vents or fruiting bodies
- Often shed continuously by both autotrophs and heterotrophs
- Chemical signalling appears to modulate receptivity of hosts

### 3. SBLUs (Symbiotic Basic Life Units)
- Small, metabolically active BLU packets that retain a fragment of their parent composite's GFP
- Can survive independently for hours to days
- May assist with:
  - Pre-processing eGFP in shared environments
  - Guarding womb entrances (as a molecular screen)
  - Enabling environmental reproduction (see [B009](B009%20%E2%80%93%20Non-Womb%20Reproduction.md))
- Found most often in tidal zones, forest canopies, and the Flying Forest microclimate

---

## Womb Partifunct Interface

GFP carriers are not absorbed indiscriminately. In womb-bearing organisms:
- Specialised interface partifuncts exist to regulate GFP entry
- Mites and SBLUs may linger near these openings for long durations
- Selective uptake appears to favour certain GFP lineages
  - Possibly guided by shared biochemical tags or hormonal gradients
  - Hypothesis: organisms are "tuning" their offspring's genetic potential by controlling GFP entry

---

## Synchronisation & Dispersion

Field data shows that many carrier organisms synchronise their dispersal to:
- Lunar events, especially Radita eclipses and Lumna-Tida alignments
- Seasonal thermal gradients, particularly near highlands or inland sea basins
- The Flying Forest's vertical migration cycle contributes to wide-scale release events

These synchronised events may produce short-lived but intense genetic surges in target populations, accelerating taxonomic variation and enabling rapid adaptation.

---

## Implications

- Carrier organisms blur the line between symbionts, gametes, and parasites
- The balance of which GFPs reach the womb may be a key driver of fitness
- Selective breeding or engineered suppression of carriers could, in theory, shift whole ecosystems
