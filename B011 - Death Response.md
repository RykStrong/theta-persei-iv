# B011 – Structural Breakdown and Death Response in Modular Composites

## Summary

This dispatch examines the biological and ecological implications of organismal death among modular composite organisms on TPIV. Despite the novelty of their construction, death among TPIV organisms appears remarkably Earth-familiar in its immediate presentation: cessation of coordinated behaviour, tissue decomposition, predator scavenging, and microbial bloom. However, dissection and field observations confirm that modular death on TPIV includes distinctive layered responses due to the independence of the constituent biological subsystems.

---

## Observations

### 1. Death is Local, Then Systemic

- Upon lethal damage (e.g. traumatic injury), individual modules (partifuncts) begin degrading independently
- Neuromotor control halts first, followed by metabolic failure in critical modules (e.g. central vascular bundles, sensor stalks)
- Some peripheral modules remain alive for several minutes or even hours post-cessation of organismal behaviour
  - These modules exhibit slow, involuntary muscular twitching or vascular closure responses

### 2. No Active Separation

- Unlike some modular Earth organisms (e.g. colonial tunicates, lichens), TPIV composites do not disassemble post-mortem
- Tissue adhesion is non-reversible; the body breaks down as a unit, not into its constituent partifuncts
- Field hypothesis: metabolic interdependence and synchronised lifecycle stage prevent post-death viability of individual modules

### 3. Spinal and Vascular Collapse

- The hexagirdle/octagirdle ring, which houses the composite spinal analog, shows clear signs of catastrophic neural collapse, often marked by a visible darkening or bio-electric flicker
- The vascular exchange organs between limbs and core rupture early, dumping oxygen-carrying fluids and leading to rapid systemic hypoxia

---

## Decomposition Dynamics

- BLU tissue breaks down via internal enzymatic autolysis, followed by rapid colonisation by external microbial forms
- Unlike Earth, several decay agents are BLU-based organisms (single-function feeders), not bacteria
- Scavenger taxa often include partial composites, which appear adapted to ingest and redistribute disassembled BLU mass

---

## Death Recognition in Ecosystem

Several local predators and scavengers exhibit strong behavioural cues triggered by death-signatures:
- Emission of volatile organic signals
- Loss of surface thermal regulation
- Cessation of rhythmic motion (especially breathing spiracles and flagella)

---

## Addendum: Environmental Cultivation

Some large omnivorous grazers have been observed dragging carcasses into wet or rotting biomes, possibly as nutrient seeding behaviour. This may represent a pre-adaptive form of ecosystem engineering, in which decomposing modular corpses promote resource cycles beneficial to future offspring.
