# B012 – Composite Life Cycles: Embryology, Morphogenesis, and Generational Divergence

## Summary

This dispatch outlines the composite organism life cycle as currently understood on TPIV. Drawing from dissections, juvenile cohort tracking, and GFP stream analysis, we now recognise that all large composite organisms undergo a multi-stage lifecycle unlike any on Earth. These stages are coordinated not through direct inheritance, but via ecological information flow, probabilistic partifunct assembly, and structural epigenetics.

Critically, offspring bear only limited anatomical similarity to parents. While taxonomic identity holds, we note wild variance in limb count, sensory structure placement, and even circulatory architecture.

---

## Lifecycle Phases of a Composite Organism

### 1. Environmental GFP Collection and Shedding
- Adult composites continuously shed and accumulate eGFP through respiration, digestion, dermal transfer, and behavioural interactions
- eGFP is sourced from fruiting bodies, pollen-analogs, aquatic microforms, and airborne mites
- Each modular subsystem within the body selectively gathers and recombines its own lineage GFP from the environment

### 2. Partifzygote Generation
- Once sufficient eGFP is gathered, each modular system recombines with selected GFP and forms a partifzygote
- These partifzygotes function like gametes, but are structurally complete instructions for that module (e.g., limb, respiratory tract)
- They migrate to the internal reproductive zone — a dedicated womb partifunct — where environmental cues and internal hormone gradients guide further action

### 3. Assembly-Conception
- The womb orchestrates the modular compilation of the new organism
- A compatible cluster of partifzygotes reaches a critical metabolic threshold, after which coordinated epigenetic structuring begins
- During this phase:
  - Developmental patterning is initiated based on parent phenotype and womb signalling
  - Incompatible or surplus partifzygotes are reabsorbed or discarded
  - Final composite configuration emerges as an ecologically viable phenotype, not a genetic clone

> *"The children don't look much like their parents — only mostly same species and few family traits at all."*
> — Dr. Joaquin Mendes, on field cohort 3A-J

### 4. Embryonic Growth and Birth
- The new organism develops from a womb-organ tissue scaffold, similar in structure to placental tissue
- Once it achieves functional metabolic integration, it detaches and is birthed as a fully independent juvenile
- Organisms are viable at birth, though often display residual growth zones and underdeveloped modules

### 5. Juvenile Maturation
- Juveniles rapidly mature by:
  - Completing delayed tissue expression
  - Consuming GFP-rich environments to finalise internal modular populations
- Some modules remain semi-plastic, continuing to evolve minor structures during early life stages
- Growth asymmetry and intrapopulation diversity are normal features

## Evolutionary and Taxonomic Notes

Taxon tracking must rely on behavioural, metabolic, and ecological metrics, not morphology alone.

**PSV (Partifunct Set Variance):** How consistent a species' internal partifunct lineup is across individuals.
- Example: Zebra Grazer = PSV 84.6%

**MDI (Morphological Divergence Index):** Ranks visual and structural variance from 0–10+
- Example: Zebra Grazer = MDI 4.8

---

## Conclusion

The lifecycle of TPIV composites is shaped by recombinatory ecology, modular reproductive design, and environmental genomic blending. No two offspring are identical, yet taxonomic continuity persists across generations. This unique combination of heritable pattern, random combination, and ecological conditioning presents both a challenge and opportunity for evolutionary modelling.
