# B017 – Distributed Organisms: Definition, Structure, and Ecological Implications

## Summary

This dispatch formally defines and characterises **Distributed Organisms (DOs)** — a form of modular life native to TPIV in which distinct, independently motile entities (Partivants) comprise a single reproductive and ecological individual. This discovery marks a fundamental departure from terrestrial models of individuality, though it bears great similarity to eusocial behaviour as seen in Earth's hive-based insects.

---

## I. Formal Definition

**Distributed Organism (DO):**
> A biologically integrated system composed of multiple semi-autonomous motile units (Partivants) that together constitute a single reproductive entity. All Partivants share a synchronised reproductive cycle, behaviourally and genetically coordinated via specialised information transfer systems (e.g., DO-mites).

---

## II. Structural Components

**Partivant *(n.)*:**
> A viable, often mobile biological unit within a DO that expresses one or more ecological, reproductive, or behavioural functions.
- Metabolically dependent upon group
- Reproductively incomplete
- Behaviourally synchronised with collective identity

**DO-mites:**
Insect-like aerial or swimming carriers that transfer Cognition Exchange Packages (CEP) between partivants.
- Fly at high speed between nodes
- Contest airspace with rival DOs
- Transmit behavioural, hormonal, and tactical information
- Often specialised by caste or communication bandwidth

---

## III. Key Traits and Behaviours

1. **Shared Genetic Architecture** — Each partivant carries a subset of the DO's total partifunct loadout; whole-genome functionality only emerges across the distributed system
2. **Unified Reproductive Output** — Only designated reproductive partivants (usually centralised) participate in offspring generation; most others are sterile
3. **Behavioural Synchrony** — Coordinated action across dozens or hundreds of units; environmental triggers result in rapid, system-wide responses
4. **Ecological Cohesion** — DOs typically occupy a single trophic level; partivants are adapted for division of labour

---

## IV. Known DO Examples

### Horse Do-Grazer (TP-HDG-9A)
- **Trophic Role:** Large-bodied herbivore DO
- **Partivants:** 6–20 grazers, coordinated for movement, foraging, and alert behaviour
- **DO-mite Behaviour:** Dense air swarm, visibly swirling between members during stress events
- **Reproduction:** One reproductive node per unit, others sterile
- **Observed On:** Apanon continent

### Apex DO-Predator (TP-ADP-6C)
- **Trophic Role:** Pack-hunting carnivore
- **Partivants:** 3–7 large-bodied hunters with complex coordination
- **DO-mites:** Known to engage in hostile airspace contests with other predators
- **Tactic:** Surround-and-isolate manoeuvres, followed by synchronised strike
- **Observed On:** Kolofluke range

---

## V. Distinctions from Other Systems

| Feature | DO | SB | TMC (JB) |
|---------|----|----|----------|
| Reproductive Unit | Shared (centralised) | Parent composite only | Each node is reproductive |
| Autonomy | Semi-autonomous nodes | Fully dependent units | Fully autonomous nodes |
| Communication | CEP via DO-mites | None or direct | Passive/chemical |
| Duration of Assembly | Permanent | Temporary (minutes–hours) | Temporary (hours–days) |
| Genetic Architecture | Distributed | Unified | Independent |

---

## VI. Ecological and Evolutionary Notes

- DOs blur the line between colony and individual
- Provide superior resilience in hostile or changeable environments
- May represent a midpoint between traditional modular composites and fully networked ecosystems
- Often dominate high-competition niches in Apanon and Kolofluke
