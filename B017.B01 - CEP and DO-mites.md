# B017.B01 – CEP Function and DO-mite Carriers

## Summary

This dispatch establishes the fundamental biological, cognitive, and mechanical structure of Distributed Organisms (DOs) on TPIV, focusing on the role of **Cognition Exchange Packages (CEPs)** and their aerial carriers — colloquially termed **DO-mites**. The mechanism of distributed cognition, powered by CEPs and enabled by specialised insect-like couriers, represents one of the most alien yet robust biological systems discovered in the Theta Persei system to date.

---

## Key Concepts

### 1. Distributed Organism (DO)
A biological system composed of multiple semi-independent biological nodes (partivants) that function collectively as one cognitive and behavioural unit. Each partivant is fully metabolically active and has limited autonomous decision-making ability, but depends on regular CEP exchange to remain synchronised with the whole.

- Not a colonial animal in the Earth sense
- DOs are modular cognitive entities, not simply socially organised

### 2. Partivant
A large-scale biological node of a DO — functionally equivalent to a full animal but structurally designed for integration into a larger distributed entity.

Each partivant contains:
- A specialised CEP receiver/transmitter structure
- A local memory store
- Behaviour-modulation pathways sensitive to CEP commands

### 3. Cognition Exchange Package (CEP)
A CEP is a folded biochemical data structure — a self-assembled neuroprotein packet — containing current state, behaviour triggers, and identification codes. It functions as:
- **State broadcast:** Sending environmental and physiological status
- **Command injection:** Receiving and executing behaviour directives
- **Synchronisation pulse:** Keeping rhythm with group cognition

All DO partivants are equipped with specialised partifuncts for:
- **PKW (Package Write)** — encoding local states into CEP
- **PKR (Package Read)** — decoding and responding to incoming CEP

CEPs are chemically stable in flight and are tagged with emission decay markers to prioritise freshness.

### 4. DO-mite Carrier Units
Small, highly mobile arthropod-like carriers designed to transport CEPs between partivants.

**Features:**
- Flight capability across hundreds of metres per minute
- CEP storage vesicle with secure capsule coating
- Defensive behaviours to protect airspace (e.g., jamming other DO-mites)
- Intermittent docking to exchange CEPs with recipient ports

**Behaviour:**
- Continuous relaying between all active partivants
- Redundant packet delivery to ensure consistency
- Aggressive defence of airspace from rival DO systems

---

## Physiological Integration

Each DO has a central cognitive rhythm, often chemically or hormonally defined, maintained by CEP traffic. Experimental interference with CEP exchange results in:
- Desynchronisation of movement
- Conflicting behavioural responses
- Cognitive collapse if CEP flow is blocked > 5 minutes

DO-mite failure can lead to temporary multi-agent drift, where each partivant begins to behave independently until resynchronisation.

---

## Implications and Evolutionary Considerations

- **Eusocial Analogue:** While DOs are not technically eusocial, their CEP-based integration serves a parallel role to hive-mind coordination — but with decentralised processing
- **Ecological Dominance:** DOs appear in multiple biomes, dominating both predator and grazer niches
- **Selective Advantage:** DOs gain flexibility, resilience, and scalability; one node can be lost or added with minimal systemic disruption
