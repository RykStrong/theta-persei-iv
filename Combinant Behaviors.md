# Combinant Behaviors

Also called 'Joined Behaviors'

**Definition:** Multiple independent organisms physically joining temporarily for cooperative tasks  
**Key Feature:** Each organism viable independently  
**Duration:** Temporary (minutes to days), then separate  
**Earth Analog:** Schooling/flocking behavior, but with physical connection

---

## What Are Combinant Behaviors?

### Temporary Fusion Strategy

**Combinant Behavior:**
Multiple physical units joining physically together, regardless of whether or not the organisms are related.

**Key Characteristics:**
- **Multiple organisms:** Each is a complete, viable [[Composite-Organisms|composite organism]]
- **Physical connection:** Bodies actually fuse/attach, not just coordinate movement
- **Temporary:** Join for specific task, then separate
- **Mutual benefit:** All participants gain advantage from fusion
- **Reversible:** Separation causes no harm

**Earth Examples (Almost):**
Schooling and flocking (murmurations) are Earth examples that are **almost** combinant behavior—coordinated movement but no physical connection.

---

## Distinction from Other Strategies

### [[DO-vs-SB-vs-JB-Comparison|JB vs DO vs SB]]

**Combinant/Joined Behaviors (JB):**
- Multiple independent organisms
- Each viable independently
- Temporary fusion for specific tasks
- Examples: Stringbirds, Ribbonfish

**[[Distributed-Organisms-Overview|Distributed Organisms (DO)]]:**
- Multiple separated bodies ([[Partivant-Definition|partivants]])
- None viable independently
- Permanent separation
- Examples: DO-ScrubDeer, rDO-Duskmantle

**[[Separated-Behaviors-and-Exovants|Separated Behaviors (SB)]]:**
- One body temporarily separates part (exovant)
- Exovant not viable independently
- Temporary separation, must rejoin
- Examples: Throwjaw, Drophunter Blimp

## Why Combinant Behaviors Evolve

### Temporary Advantages

**Cooperative Feeding:**
- Enhanced efficiency through fusion
- Example: [[Stringbirds|Stringbirds]] complete digestive tract
- Example: [[Ribbonfish|Ribbonfish]] expanded filter surface

**Defense:**
- Larger apparent size (predator deterrence)
- Coordinated defense strategies
- Shared vigilance during fusion

**Reproduction:**
- [[Environmental-GFP|eGFP]] exchange during fusion
- Genetic material trading
- Enhanced reproductive success

**Resource Exploitation:**
- Access resources impossible for single organism
- Temporary specialization
- Division of labor during fusion

## Types of Combinant Behaviors

### By Function

**Digestive Fusion:**
- Incomplete digestive systems in individuals
- Join to create complete processing pathway
- [[Stringbirds|Stringbirds]] primary example
- Food passes through multiple bodies

**Filter Fusion:**
- Expand surface area for feeding
- [[Ribbonfish|Ribbonfish]] side-by-side joining
- Stack to create dense filter mass
- Enhanced plankton capture

**Defensive Fusion:**
- Form larger, more intimidating structure
- Coordinated defense behaviors
- Temporary protection during vulnerable periods
- Separate when threat passes

**Locomotion Fusion:**
- Enhanced speed or efficiency
- Coordinated swimming/flying
- Temporary for migration or escape
- Return to independence after

## Fusion Mechanisms

### How Organisms Connect

**Physical Attachment:**
- Specialized connection points
- Mouth-to-anus (Stringbirds)
- Side-to-side (Ribbonfish)
- Gripping structures (claws, suckers, hooks)

**Chemical Signaling:**
- Recognition pheromones (identify compatible partners)
- Synchronization hormones (coordinate physiology)
- Separation signals (when to disconnect)

**Structural Integration:**
- Temporary vascular connections (some species)
- Shared respiratory surfaces (aquatic species)
- Minimal—most retain independence
- No permanent tissue fusion

---

## Duration and Frequency

### How Long and How Often

**Short-Term (Minutes to Hours):**
- Feeding events
- Escape from predators
- Quick resource exploitation
- High-frequency (daily or more)

**Medium-Term (Hours to Days):**
- Extended feeding sessions
- Migration events
- Reproductive periods
- Medium-frequency (weekly to seasonal)

**Long-Term (Days to Weeks):**
- Rare
- Extended migrations
- Environmental stress response
- Low-frequency (seasonal or annual)

## Example Taxa

### Primary Examples

**[[Stringbirds|Stringbirds]]:**
- Individual birds have incomplete digestive tracts
- Join mouth-to-anus in air
- Food passes through multiple bodies for complete digestion
- Separate after feeding
- Most studied combinant behavior

**[[Ribbonfish|Ribbonfish]]:**
- Join side-by-side for filter-feeding
- Stack in water to create large, dense filter mass
- Trap plankton food particles
- Separate after feeding session
- Aquatic combinant behavior

## Evolutionary Perspective

### Why This Strategy Succeeds

**Advantages:**
- Retain individual viability (no dependency)
- Gain temporary specialization benefits
- Flexible response to changing conditions
- No permanent overhead (unlike [[Distributed-Organisms-Overview|DOs]])

**Disadvantages:**
- Coordination complexity during fusion
- Vulnerability while joined
- Finding compatible partners
- Energy cost of connection/disconnection

**Selection Pressures:**
- Environments with variable resource availability
- Predator pressure favoring size variation
- Incomplete individual systems that benefit from cooperation
- [[Natural-Selection-on-TPIV|Multi-level selection]] on cooperation

---

## Recognition and Compatibility

### Finding Partners

**Chemical Recognition:**
- Pheromone signatures identify species/compatibility
- Individual recognition (some species)
- Genetic compatibility assessment ([[Environmental-GFP|eGFP]] exchange potential)

**Visual/Auditory Signals:**
- Color patterns indicate readiness to join
- Calls coordinate fusion events
- Flocking/schooling brings potential partners together

**Behavioral Cues:**
- Specific movements signal willingness
- Positioning for optimal connection
- Rejection behaviors (incompatible partners)

---

## Communication During Fusion

### Coordinated While Connected

**Physical Signals:**
- Muscle contractions (peristalsis in Stringbirds)
- Pressure changes
- Movement coordination

**Chemical Signals:**
- Hormones pass between connected individuals
- Metabolic coordination
- Stress signals (trigger separation if needed)

**Neural Coordination:**
- Limited in most species
- Some vascular connection allows neurotransmitter exchange
- Maintains individual [[33-Brain-Partifuncts|cognitive control]]

---

## Separation

### How and When to Disconnect

**Triggers:**
- Task completed (feeding done)
- Threat passed (defense no longer needed)
- Resource exhausted
- Chemical signal (mutual agreement)
- Stress signal (one individual threatened)

**Mechanism:**
- Muscle relaxation at connection points
- Physical detachment
- Brief metabolic adjustment
- Return to independent function

**Post-Separation:**
- No lasting connection
- May rejoin same individuals later
- Or find new partners
- Fully independent immediately

## Combinant Behaviors and DOs

### Can They Coexist?

**Yes—Not Mutually Exclusive:**
An organism can both be Distributed and have Combinant Behaviors, as they are two different things.

**Theoretical Scenario:**
- [[Distributed-Organisms-Overview|DO]] organism (multiple [[Partivant-Definition|partivants]])
- Some partivants capable of temporarily fusing with other organisms (JB)
- Example: DO feeding partivants join with non-DO filter feeders
- Rare but not impossible

**No Known Examples:**
- Not yet observed in field
- Theoretically viable
- Would require extremely complex coordination
- High [[3B-Womb-Partifunct-Systems|womb]] assembly challenge

## Research Observations

### Field Studies

**Frequency:**
- Combinant behaviors common in aquatic environments
- Less common terrestrial (Stringbirds notable exception)
- Aerial combinant behaviors rare but exist

**Species Diversity:**
- 50+ documented combinant species
- [[Stringbirds|Stringbirds]] most studied
- [[Ribbonfish|Ribbonfish]] second most studied
- Many others less well understood

**Ecological Impact:**
- Significant in plankton control (Ribbonfish)
- Aerial insect population regulation (Stringbirds)
- Nutrient cycling (cooperative feeding)

## Comparative Notes

### Earth Analogs

**Closest Earth Behaviors:**
- Schooling fish (coordination without connection)
- Flocking birds (murmurations)
- Slime mold aggregation (single-celled, but fusion)
- Biofilm bacteria (sessile fusion)

**TPIV Unique Aspects:**
- Physical connection in mobile organisms
- Complete digestive tract assembly (Stringbirds)
- Large-scale filter construction (Ribbonfish)
- Reversible fusion in complex organisms
