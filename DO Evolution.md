1. Pre-DO: Super-Guilds and Proto-Distributed Systems
On TPIV, it is easy to get this sequence:
2. Normal composite guilds
o Browsers and pack hunters coordinating via scent, sound, visual displays.
o Shared eGFP environments, overlap in partifunct lineages, occasional symbioses.
3. Super-guilds / quasi-colonies
o Long-term, multi-generational associations:
* Browsers that preferentially recombine with certain scout or sentinel lineages.
* Hunters that coordinate tightly with a small group of scouts and chasers.
o You get something that behaves almost like a DO, but:
* Each body is still viable alone.
* Reproduction is mostly per-composite organism, not per-distributed system.
3. Proto-DOs
o Certain roles become so specialized that their partifunct assemblages:
* Lose independent survival capacity.
* Rely on shared digestion, shared reproduction, shared DO-mites.
o At this point you have a system on the cusp:
* Either it collapses, or
* Selection locks it in as a true Distributed Organism.
Predator�prey competition is very good at deciding which proto-DO systems get locked in.

2. Apanon: DO ScrubDeer Browser vs DO HillWolf Pack Hunter

A composite browser guild in that setting hits several problems:
* No single composite can see enough of the terrain to monitor predators and forage quality.
* Herding behavior is limited by:
o Line-of-sight issues.
o Variable cover.
o Short-range chemical and acoustic signaling.
A proto-distributed browser system gains:
* Wide-area sampling of scrub patches via spatially separated browsing nodes.

* Redundancy: losing any single browser node to predation hurts, but does not destroy the organism.
Under heavy predation pressure, we expect selection to favor:
* Tight integration of:
o Browse-partivants (feeders).

4. Did Competition Create the DOs?
So, to answer your question precisely:
* No, competition did not create DO biology from scratch. That requires:
o Partifunct-level modularity.
o DO-mites.
o Existing tendencies toward shared digestion and shared reproduction.
Those are features of TPIV�s baseline biology.
* Yes, competition did help create specific DO lineages, in the sense that:
o It pushed super-guilds and proto-DO systems across the threshold into:
* Obligatory integration.
* Partivants that are not viable outside the organism.
o It selected strongly for:
* Wide-area sensory and control networks.
* High redundancy and resilience.
* Expensive coordination infrastructure that only makes sense if the whole system is treated as a single organism.
In other words:
* The environment and partifunct architecture make DOs possible.
* The ScrubDeer vs HillWolf and rDO SpiderRabbit vs rDO Duskmantle arms races make specific DO solutions locally optimal, but only in a handful of extreme niches.
That�s why DOs stay rare but spectacular, and why those particular browser�pack-hunter pairings are such important �origin stories� in-universe for how far TPIV modular life can be pushed.
