# DO vs SB vs JB Comparison

**Three Advanced Strategies:** Distributed, Separated, and Joined/Combinant behaviors  
**Key Distinction:** Viability and permanence of separation  
**Common Thread:** All involve physical separation or fusion of body parts  
**Different Solutions:** Each strategy solves different ecological challenges

---

## Overview

### Three Organizational Patterns

An organism can both be Distributed and have Combinant Behaviors, as they are two different things.

**The Three Strategies:**

1. **Distributed Organisms (DO/rDO):** One organism in many permanently separated bodies (partivants)
2. **Separated Behaviors (SB):** One organism temporarily separates a part (exovant)
3. **Joined/Combinant Behaviors (JB):** Multiple independent organisms temporarily fuse

---

## Distributed Organisms (DO/rDO)

### One Organism, Many Bodies

**[Definition](Distributed%20Organisms%20Overview.md):**
A Distributed Organism is made up of **[partivants](Partivant%20Definition.md)**—multiple physically separated bodies that function collectively as a single organism.

**Key Features:**
- **Physical State:** Multiple separated bodies
- **Viability:** None of the partivants are viable outside the DO
- **Duration:** Permanent separation (from birth/assembly)
- **Communication:** [CEP signals](CEP%20and%20Do-Mites.md), do-mites, or radio waves (rDO)
- **Identity:** Single organism distributed across multiple bodies

**Examples:**
- **[DO-ScrubDeer Browser](DO%20ScrubDeer%20Browser.md)**: Feeding partivants + digestive partivants + sensory partivants
- **[rDO-Duskmantle-Hunter](rDO%20Duskmantle%20Stalker.md)**: Scout partivants + attack partivants + feeding partivants
- **[DO-Horse Grazer](Distributed%20Organisms%20Overview.md)**: Grazing partivants + processors + scouts

**Analogy:**
Like an anthill—one organism in many separated bodies, none viable alone.

## Separated Behaviors (SB)

### Temporary Detachment

**[Definition](Separated%20Behaviors%20and%20Exovants.md):**
Organism temporarily separates a part of itself (called an **exovant**) to perform a limited task, then rejoins.

**Key Features:**
- **Physical State:** One body temporarily separates chunk
- **Viability:** The detached exovant is NOT viable on its own
- **Duration:** Temporary (seconds to hours), must rejoin
- **Communication:** Often minimal (pre-programmed behavior)
- **Identity:** Single organism with detachable parts

**Exovant Types:**

**Low-Function Exovants:**
- Simple, pre-programmed actions
- Example: **[Throwjaw](Separated%20Behaviors%20and%20Exovants.md)** "jaw" (bite and release)
- Example: **[Tossclaw](Separated%20Behaviors%20and%20Exovants.md)** claw (grab and retrieve)

**High-Function Exovants:**
- Complex, autonomous behavior
- Example: **[Drophunter Blimp](Separated%20Behaviors%20and%20Exovants.md)** hunter exovant (fly, hunt, kill, return)

**Analogy:**
Like a lizard dropping its tail, but the tail comes back.

---

## Joined/Combinant Behaviors (JB)

### Temporary Fusion

**[Definition](Combinant%20Behaviors.md):**
Multiple independent organisms physically join together temporarily to perform a task cooperatively, then separate.

**Key Features:**
- **Physical State:** Multiple organisms fuse together
- **Viability:** Each organism is viable independently
- **Duration:** Temporary (minutes to days), then separate
- **Communication:** During fusion, shared resources/signals
- **Identity:** Multiple organisms cooperating

**Examples:**
- **[Stringbirds](Stringbirds.md)**: Individual birds join mouth-to-anus to create complete digestive tract
- **[Ribbonfish](Ribbonfish.md)**: Join side-by-side for filter-feeding, stack in water to trap plankton

**Earth Analogy:**
Schooling fish or flocking birds (almost combinant behavior), but TPIV organisms physically connect.

## Comparison Table

| **Feature** | **DO/rDO** | **SB** | **JB** |
|-------------|-----------|--------|--------|
| **Physical Units** | Multiple partivants | Single body + exovant | Multiple organisms |
| **Separation** | Permanent | Temporary | Temporary fusion |
| **Individual Viability** | None viable alone | Exovant not viable | Each viable alone |
| **Communication** | CEP/mites/radio (constant) | Minimal/pre-programmed | Shared during fusion |
| **Purpose** | Resource distribution, specialization | Task execution (hunting, defense) | Cooperative feeding, defense |
| **Rejoining** | Never joined | Must rejoin | Can separate anytime |
| **Examples** | ScrubDeer, HillWolf | Throwjaw, Drophunter | Stringbirds, Ribbonfish |

## Viability Spectrum

### The Critical Distinction

**Cannot Survive Independently:**
- **DO partivants**: None viable (permanently separated)
- **SB exovants**: Not viable (must rejoin parent)

**Can Survive Independently:**
- **JB organisms**: Each fully viable (join temporarily)

**Visual Representation:**
```
DO:  [Body A] ← not viable → [Body B] ← not viable → [Body C]
     └─────────── One Organism ──────────┘

SB:  [Main Body] ←─ must rejoin ─→ [Exovant]
     └─────────── One Organism ──────────┘

JB:  [Organism A] ←─ temporary ─→ [Organism B] ←─ temporary ─→ [Organism C]
         viable           fusion          viable          fusion        viable
```

## Why Each Strategy Exists

### Distributed Organisms (DO/rDO)

**Ecological Niche:**
- Patchy resources over wide area
- Need for extreme specialization
- Environments where distribution advantageous
- [Island continents](Continents.md) with limited partifunct diversity

**Trade-offs:**
- High overhead (communication energy)
- Complex assembly (specialized partivants)
- Benefits: Resource exploitation, resilience, flexibility

---

### Separated Behaviors (SB)

**Ecological Niche:**
- Tasks requiring temporary extension beyond body
- Hunting strategies (drop predation)
- Defense mechanisms (deployable weapons)
- Situations where full body presence disadvantageous

**Trade-offs:**
- Risk of exovant loss
- Energy to regenerate/rejoin
- Benefits: Extended reach, specialized attacks, minimal risk to main body

---

### Joined Behaviors (JB)

**Ecological Niche:**
- Tasks requiring cooperation
- Incomplete individual systems (Stringbirds)
- Enhanced efficiency through fusion (Ribbonfish)
- Temporary benefits without permanent commitment

**Trade-offs:**
- Coordination during fusion
- Vulnerability while joined
- Benefits: Shared resources, cooperative feeding, enhanced capability

---

## Can Strategies Combine?

### Yes - Not Mutually Exclusive

**DO + JB Possible:**
An organism can both be Distributed and have Combinant Behaviors, as they are two different things.

**Example Scenario:**
- DO organism (multiple partivants)
- Some partivants can temporarily fuse with other organisms (JB)
- Rare but theoretically possible

**DO + SB Unlikely:**
- Partivants already incomplete
- Separating exovant from partivant would leave both non-viable
- No known examples

**SB + JB Possible:**
- Standard organism with SB capability
- Can also temporarily join with others
- No known examples, but not impossible

## Evolutionary Relationships

### Independent Origins

**Not Linear Evolution:**
- DO, SB, and JB did not evolve from each other
- Each strategy evolved independently
- Convergent solutions to different problems
- [Modular biology](Non%20Homochirality.md) enables all three

**Shared Foundation:**
- All rely on [partifunct](Partifunct%20Core%20Definition.md) modularity
- All use [eGFP](Environmental%20GFP.md) for reproduction
- All assembled by [womb](Womb%20Partifunct%20Systems.md)
- All benefit from [multi-level selection](Natural%20Selection%20on%20TPIV.md)

## Earth Analogs

### Closest Comparisons

**DO (Distributed Organisms):**
- Social insects (ants, bees) - BUT all genetically identical
- Siphonophores - BUT physically connected
- No true Earth analog

**SB (Separated Behaviors):**
- Lizard tail autotomy - BUT doesn't rejoin
- Octopus arm autonomy - BUT doesn't rejoin
- No true Earth analog

**JB (Joined Behaviors):**
- Schooling/flocking - CLOSE, but no physical connection
- Biofilm bacteria - CLOSE, but simpler
- Slime mold aggregation - CLOSEST analog

## Research Observations

### Field Identification

**How to Tell Them Apart:**

**DO Indicators:**
- Cloud of do-mites visible
- Coordinated behavior across separated bodies
- Individual bodies incomplete (missing organs)
- Genetic testing shows same organism

**SB Indicators:**
- Detachment and rejoining observed
- Exovant has pre-programmed behavior
- Main body waits for return
- Clear attachment points

**JB Indicators:**
- Multiple complete organisms observed separately
- Temporary physical fusion
- Separation without harm
- Each organism viable alone

## Comparative Fitness

### When Each Strategy Wins

**DO Advantages:**
- Patchy resources
- Need for wide-area coverage
- Extreme specialization benefits
- Predator resistance (diffuse target)

**SB Advantages:**
- Specific hunting strategies
- Extended reach needed temporarily
- Risk to main body minimized
- Flexibility without permanent commitment

**JB Advantages:**
- Temporary cooperative tasks
- Enhanced efficiency through fusion
- No permanent overhead
- Maintains individual autonomy
