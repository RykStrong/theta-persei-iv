Day 12. Witnessed inter-DO conflict—first observation of attack/defense 
mite deployment in field conditions.

Two DO-Hillwolf packs encountered at territorial boundary. Escalating 
aggression. Then mite warfare.

**Initial Encounter (T+0:00):**

Pack Alpha (our study pack): 8 partivants, established territory
Pack Beta (intruders): 6 partivants, expanding range

Visual contact at 200m. Both packs show agitation (posture, vocalizations).

Communication mite traffic increases dramatically (both packs coordinating 
responses internally).

**Territorial Display Phase (T+2:00 to T+8:00):**

Both packs: Aggressive displays, posturing, boundary marking
Communication mites: Heavy traffic within each pack
Sensory mites: Deployed by both sides (scouting enemy positions)

Observed: Beta pack sensory mites flying toward Alpha territory
Alpha defense mites: INTERCEPT

First mite-vs-mite engagement visible at 150m range (binoculars):
- Alpha defense mites (standard size, ~6mm) patrol territory perimeter
- Beta sensory mites (micro size, ~2mm) approach
- Defense mites intercept, mid-air collisions
- Several Beta sensory mites destroyed, fall to ground
- Remaining Beta sensory mites retreat

**Analysis:**
Alpha pack has functional defense mite capability. Prevents enemy 
reconnaissance.

**Escalation Phase (T+10:00):**

Beta coordinator (identified by heavy communication mite traffic): 
Produces different mite type.

New mites visible: Larger (~8-10mm), different flight pattern (aggressive, 
fast, direct), darker coloration.

Attack mites (FIRST FIELD OBSERVATION).

Beta attack mites: Swarm formation (30-40 individuals), flying toward 
Alpha pack scout partivant (isolated position on ridge).

Alpha response: Defense mite surge from scout partivant + reinforcements 
from nearby partivants.

**Mite Battle Observable at 120m Range:**

Attack swarm vs. defense screen—visible as chaotic cloud of small insects.

Combat behavior:
- Attack mites aggressive, diving at defense mites
- Defense mites blocking, grappling, some fall to ground locked together
- Casualties both sides (dozens of dead mites visible on ground post-battle)

Duration: 3-4 minutes of intense mite combat

Result: Beta attack partially successful—some attack mites penetrate 
defense, reach Alpha scout partivant.

**Scout Partivant Attack (T+14:30):**

Beta attack mites land on Alpha scout. Scout shows distress behavior:
- Rapid movement, attempting to dislodge mites
- Visible agitation
- Communication mite production surges (alarm CEP to pack?)

Attack duration: ~90 seconds before scout successfully dislodges/destroys 
attacking mites

Post-attack: Scout retreats from exposed position, moves toward pack 
protection

**Pack-Level Response (T+16:00):**

Alpha coordinator: Massive mite production event

Multiple mite types launching:
- Communication mites: Coordinating pack-wide response
- Defense mites: Reinforcing all partivant defenses
- Attack mites: ALPHA PRODUCES ATTACK MITES (counter-offensive)

Alpha attack mites (40-50 individuals): Swarm formation, fly toward 
Beta pack

Beta response: Deploy own defense mites

Second mite battle: Mid-territory, ~150m from both packs

Even larger scale: 80-100 mites engaged, multiple overlapping combats

**Stalemate (T+22:00):**

Both packs: Heavy defense mite screens deployed
Attack mites: Unable to penetrate enemy defenses
Communication mites: Still functioning (both packs maintain cognitive 
integration despite conflict)

Neither pack gaining advantage. High cost (mite production energy 
expensive, casualties significant).

**Conflict Resolution (T+35:00):**

Beta pack: Withdraws from territory boundary
Alpha pack: Does not pursue, reinforces defensive positions

Combat ends. Both packs return to normal operations.

Post-conflict observation:
- Ground littered with dead DO-mites (estimated 200-300 casualties both 
  sides)
- Both packs show reduced mite traffic (depleted production capacity, 
  energy conservation)
- Recovery time: ~4 hours before normal mite traffic density restored

**Critical Observations:**

1. **Attack mites are real**: Not theoretical, actively deployed in 
   territorial conflicts

2. **Defense mites are essential**: Pack without defense mites would 
   suffer severe disadvantage (reconnaissance prevented, communication 
   disrupted, partivants vulnerable)

3. **Mite warfare is costly**: High energy expenditure, significant 
   casualties, affects pack operations for hours post-conflict

4. **Cognitive warfare**: Attack mites target enemy communication 
   networks—destroy DO-mites in flight = disrupt enemy coordination

5. **Escalation dynamics**: Conflict progressed from display → sensory 
   probing → attack → counter-attack → stalemate → withdrawal

6. **Strategic targeting**: Attack mites target vulnerable partivants 
   (scouts, sensory network)—disable enemy sensing = tactical advantage

**Evolutionary Implications:**

DO populations in high-competition environments show:
- Higher investment in attack/defense mite production
- More sophisticated mite combat capabilities
- Coordinated swarm tactics (not just individual mite combat)

DO populations in low-competition environments:
- Minimal attack mite investment (unnecessary expense)
- Basic defense mites only (protect against parasites/opportunistic threats)
- Resources invested in feeding/reproduction instead

This is arms race dynamics: Attack capability selects for defense 
capability, defense selects for better attack, repeat.

Populations locked in evolutionary feedback loop, producing increasingly 
sophisticated mite warfare systems.
