Despite the novelty of their construction, death among TPIV organisms appears remarkably **Earth-familiar** in its immediate presentation: cessation of coordinated behavior, tissue decomposition, predator scavenging, and microbial bloom; this in an otherwise alien biosphere.

However, dissection and field observations confirm that **modular death** on TPIV includes **distinctive layered responses** due to the independence of the constituent biological subsystems.

### **Observations**

#### **1. Death is Local, Then Systemic**

- Upon lethal damage (e.g. traumatic injury), **individual modules** (partifuncts) begin degrading independently.
- Neuromotor control halts first, followed by metabolic failure in critical modules (e.g. central vascular bundles, sensor stalks).
-  Some peripheral modules remain alive for several minutes or even hours post-cessation of organismal behavior.
- These modules exhibit slow, involuntary muscular twitching or vascular closure responses.

#### **2. No Active Separation**

- Unlike some modular Earth organisms (e.g. colonial tunicates, lichens), TPIV composites **do not disassemble** post-mortem.
- Tissue adhesion is **non-reversible**; the body breaks down as a unit, not into its constituent partifuncts.
- Metabolic interdependence and synchronized lifecycle stage prevent post-death viability of individual modules.

#### **3. Spinal and Vascular Collapse**

- The **hexagirdle/octagirdle ring**, which houses the composite spinal analog, shows clear signs of catastrophic neural collapse, often marked by a visible darkening or bio-electric flicker.
- The **vascular exchange organs** between limbs and core rupture early, dumping oxygen-carrying fluids and leading to rapid systemic hypoxia.

### **Decomposition Dynamics**

- **BLU tissue** (TPIV’s analog to cellular structure) breaks down via internal enzymatic autolysis, followed by rapid colonization by external microbial forms.
- Decay agents are **BLU-based organisms** (single-function feeders)
- Scavenger taxa often include both **high and low PH solvents**, which appear adapted to ingest and redistribute disassembled BLU mass.

### **Death Recognition in Ecosystem**

- Several local predators and scavengers exhibit **strong behavioral cues** triggered by death-signatures:
- Emission of volatile organic signals.
- Loss of surface thermal regulation.
- Cessation of rhythmic motion (especially breathing spiracles and flagella).
