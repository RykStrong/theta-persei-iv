# Ribbonfish

**Classification:** Aquatic Filter Feeder (Combinant Behavior)  
**Biome:** Oceans, large lakes—pelagic and coastal zones  
**Fusion Type:** Side-by-side filter expansion  
**Individual Viability:** Fully independent  
**Fusion Duration:** Hours to days (feeding sessions)

---

## Overview

### Aquatic Filter Fusion

**Ribbonfish** are aquatic organisms that engage in [[Combinant-Behaviors|combinant behavior]] by joining side-by-side to create large, dense filter masses for enhanced plankton capture.

**Key Features:**
- Each individual is a complete, viable organism
- Join side-by-side (lateral fusion)
- Stack in water to trap plankton food particles
- Create expanded filter surface area
- Separate after feeding session

**The Name:**
Called "Ribbonfish" because individuals are elongated, ribbon-like in shape, and when joined create flowing ribbon-like structures in the water.

---

## Individual Anatomy

### Complete but Enhanced by Fusion

**Body Structure:**
- Elongated, flattened body (ribbon-like)
- 30-120 cm length typical (varies by species)
- Thin profile (2-5 cm width)
- Flexible skeleton ([[37a-Lignin-vs-Calcium-Bones|lignin-based]])
- Multiple [[Respiratory-Systems|gill structures]] along sides

**Solo Filter Feeding:**
- Mouth: Large, forward-facing
- Internal filter structures (baleen-like)
- Water intake → filter → expel
- Can survive independently
- But lower efficiency than when joined

**Swimming:**
- Undulating body motion (eel-like)
- Multiple fin structures (dorsal, ventral, lateral)
- Can swim horizontally or vertically
- Moderate speed (not pursuit predator)

---

## Fusion Mechanism

### Side-by-Side Connection

**Connection Points:**
- Specialized lateral attachment structures (along body sides)
- Hook-and-groove system
- Muscular grip maintains connection
- Multiple attachment points per individual (5-10 along length)

**Physical Process:**
1. Individuals align parallel to each other
2. Approach slowly (swimming in same direction)
3. Lateral structures interlock (hooks engage grooves)
4. Muscle contraction secures connection
5. Additional ribbonfish join on either side
6. Form vertical stack or horizontal sheet

**Formation Types:**

**Vertical Stack (Most Common):**
- Ribbonfish stacked one above another
- Creates curtain-like structure in water
- 5-30 individuals typical
- Vertical orientation in water column

**Horizontal Sheet (Less Common):**
- Ribbonfish arranged in horizontal plane
- Creates carpet-like structure
- 3-10 individuals typical
- Used in shallower water

---

## Filter Feeding Strategy

### Enhanced Capture Efficiency

**Solo Feeding:**
- Individual swims through plankton-rich water
- Opens mouth, water flows through internal filters
- Plankton trapped, water expelled
- Efficiency: ~30-40% of plankton captured

**Joined Feeding:**
Ribbonfish join side-by-side for filter-feeding, stack in water to create large, dense filter mass to trap plankton food particles.

**Vertical Stack Process:**
1. Stack forms perpendicular to current
2. Water flows through gaps between individuals
3. Each ribbonfish filters water passing through
4. Plankton trapped at multiple layers
5. Very little plankton escapes stack
6. Efficiency: ~80-90% capture rate

**Water Flow:**
- Current pushes water through stack
- Or coordinated swimming creates flow
- Plankton concentrated by multiple filter layers
- Dense aggregations quickly depleted

---

## Coordination While Joined

### Moving as One

**Swimming Coordination:**
- **Lead individual** (usually top or front) sets direction
- Others follow via physical connection
- Synchronized undulations (wave passes through stack)
- Slower than individual swimming (drag from formation)

**Communication:**
- Physical tension signals (pull = direction change)
- Chemical signals through contact (stress hormones)
- Visual cues (color changes along body)
- Vibration sensing (detect partner movements)

**Flexibility:**
- Stack can bend and flex (navigate around obstacles)
- Individual control maintained (each can adjust positioning)
- Break formation if needed (emergency separation)

---

## Feeding Session Duration

### Hours to Days

**Short Sessions (Hours):**
- Plankton bloom encounters (intense but brief)
- High-density feeding areas
- Quick satiation
- Separate to digest and rest

**Long Sessions (Days):**
- Persistent plankton zones (upwelling areas, river mouths)
- Continuous slow feeding
- Individuals rotate positions (share lead effort)
- Sleep while joined (reduced individuals maintain swimming)

**Record Duration:**
- Longest observed: 6 days continuous
- Large stack (23 individuals)
- Seasonal plankton bloom exploitation
- Eventually separated due to predator threat

---

## Separation

### When and How to Disconnect

**Triggers:**
- Feeding area depleted (plankton gone)
- Digestive capacity reached (full)
- Predator detected (threat)
- Weather change (storm approaching)
- Individual stress (injury, exhaustion)
- Chemical signal (mutual agreement)

**Process:**
1. Signal initiated (usually by lead individual)
2. Muscular relaxation at attachment points
3. Hooks disengage from grooves
4. Progressive separation (top to bottom or simultaneous)
5. Brief period of coordinated swimming (stay near)
6. Gradual dispersal

**Post-Separation:**
- No lasting connection
- Can rejoin same individuals later (recognition)
- Can join different groups
- Fully independent immediately

---

## Individual Survival

### Viable Alone

**Solo Life:**
- Each ribbonfish is complete [[Composite-Organisms|composite organism]]
- All systems functional independently
- Can feed, survive, reproduce alone
- But with reduced efficiency

**Solo Challenges:**
- Lower filter efficiency (miss more plankton)
- Must feed longer to reach satiation
- More vulnerable to predators (no group size advantage)
- Lower reproductive success (less energy surplus)

**When Solo:**
- Young individuals (learning to join)
- Post-injury (healing attachment structures)
- Low population density (no partners available)
- Digesting after long feeding session

---

## Social Dynamics

### Finding Partners

**Recognition:**
- Species-specific bioluminescent patterns
- Chemical signatures (contact pheromones)
- Size matching (similar length preferred)
- Visual assessment (color, condition)

**Compatibility:**
- Same species strongly preferred (best fit)
- Similar size optimal (matching swimming speed)
- Healthy individuals favored (avoid disease)
- Experienced "lead" individuals sought (better coordination)

**Group Formation:**
- Individuals gather at known feeding zones
- Visual/chemical cues attract others
- First pair joins, others add sequentially
- Optimal stack size: 8-15 individuals
- Too large: Coordination difficult
- Too small: Limited efficiency gain

---

## Reproduction

### Independent Process

**No Reproductive Fusion:**
- Mating occurs independently
- [[Environmental-GFP|eGFP]] exchange during close swimming (not joined)
- [[3B-Womb-Partifunct-Systems|Womb partifunct]] in each individual
- Live birth typical

**Fusion Benefits Reproduction:**
- Better nutrition (efficient feeding)
- More energy for offspring development
- Healthier neonates
- Higher survival rate
- Enhanced [[Environmental-GFP|eGFP]] diversity (more plankton = more genetic sources)

**Offspring:**
- Born with attachment structures
- Learn joining behavior by observing adults
- Practice joining with siblings
- Full competence by 6-12 months (species-dependent)

---

## Predator Interactions

### Group Defense

**Threats:**
- Large aquatic predators (ambush hunters)
- [[46c-Drophunter-Blimp|Aerial predators]] (surface attacks)
- Pursuit predators (fast swimmers)

**Defense Strategies:**
- **Apparent size:** Stack looks like single large organism (deterrent)
- **Quick separation:** Emergency disconnect (<2 seconds)
- **Scatter:** All directions simultaneously (predator confusion)
- **Coordinated evasion:** Stack maneuvers as unit (if time permits)

**Individual Vulnerability:**
- Separated ribbonfish more vulnerable
- Predators target stragglers
- Rejoining occurs quickly after threat

---

## Plankton Dynamics

### Ecosystem Impact

**Heavy Feeding Pressure:**
- Large stacks can deplete local plankton rapidly
- 20-ribbonfish stack: Clear 100m³ water in hours
- Significant ecosystem impact in feeding zones
- Population control on plankton blooms

**Seasonal Patterns:**
- Follow plankton bloom cycles
- Migrations track food availability
- Aggregations at upwelling zones
- Concentration near river mouths (nutrient input)

**Trophic Role:**
- Transfer plankton biomass to higher trophic levels
- Prey for larger predators
- [[41-Trophic-Roles-No-Plant-Animal|Omnivore]] technically (plankton includes animal and plant analogs)

---

## Geographic Distribution

### Pelagic and Coastal

**Oceanic:**
- Open ocean plankton zones
- Follow currents and blooms
- Pelagic migratory patterns
- Vertical distribution (surface to 200m depth)

**Coastal:**
- Upwelling zones (nutrient-rich)
- River mouths (freshwater plankton input)
- Reef edges (plankton concentration)
- Seasonal blooms (spring/fall typical)

**Freshwater (Some Species):**
- Large lakes (Great Lakes analogs)
- Slow-moving rivers
- Reservoir systems
- Generally smaller body size than marine species

## Evolutionary Perspective

### Why Side-by-Side Fusion?

**Advantages:**
- Dramatically increased filter efficiency (3× solo)
- Reduced energy per unit food (swimming shared)
- Predator deterrence (apparent size)
- Social information (group finds food better)

**Trade-offs:**
- Coordination overhead
- Attachment structure maintenance
- Vulnerability while joined (harder to escape quickly)
- Competition for same plankton patch

**Selection Pressure:**
- Plankton patchiness favors group feeding
- [[Natural-Selection-on-TPIV|Multi-level selection]] on cooperation
- Cultural transmission (learned behavior)
- Environments with variable plankton density

## Research Observations

### Field Studies

**First Observation:**
*"We thought it was a kelp forest—vertical ribbons swaying in current. Then the entire structure swam away. Twenty-three individuals, stacked like books on a shelf, moving as one fluid curtain through the water."*  
— Marine Survey Log, Day 67

**Feeding Efficiency:**
- Solo: ~35% plankton capture
- 3-ribbonfish: ~60% capture
- 8-ribbonfish: ~85% capture
- 15-ribbonfish: ~92% capture
- Diminishing returns beyond 15

**Formation Stability:**
- Small stacks (3-5): Very stable, hours to days
- Medium stacks (8-12): Stable, moderate duration
- Large stacks (15+): Less stable, shorter duration
- Maximum observed: 31 individuals (lasted 45 minutes only)

## Comparative Notes

### Similar to Stringbirds

**Similarities:**
- Both [[Combinant-Behaviors|combinant behaviors]]
- Both enhance feeding efficiency
- Both fully viable independently
- Both temporary fusion

**Differences:**
- Ribbonfish: Aquatic, side-by-side, filter feeding
- [[Stringbirds|Stringbirds]]: Aerial, sequential, digestive fusion
- Ribbonfish: Relatively stationary while joined (drift or slow swim)
- Stringbirds: Highly mobile while joined (active flight)
