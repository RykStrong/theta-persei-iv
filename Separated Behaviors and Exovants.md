# Separated Behaviors and Exovants

**Definition:** Organism temporarily separates a part (exovant) to perform limited task  
**Key Feature:** Exovant NOT viable independently—must rejoin  
**Duration:** Temporary (seconds to hours)  
**Critical Distinction:** Exovant ≠ Partivant (exovant must rejoin, partivant permanently separated)

---

## What Are Separated Behaviors?

### Temporary Detachment Strategy

**Separated Behavior:**
Organism will temporarily separate a part of itself (the **exovant**) to perform a limited task, then the exovant must rejoin.

**Key Characteristics:**
- **One organism:** Single [[Composite-Organisms|composite organism]] with detachable part
- **Temporary separation:** Seconds to hours maximum
- **Exovant not viable:** Cannot survive independently
- **Must rejoin:** Parent body waits for/retrieves exovant
- **Pre-programmed behavior:** Exovant has limited, specific function
- Enabled by a 'separation' partifunct

---

## What is an Exovant?

### The Detached Chunk

**[[Partivant-Definition|Exovant vs Partivant]]:**

**Exovant (in Separated Behaviors):**
- Temporarily separated chunk from single body
- NOT viable independently
- Must rejoin parent organism
- Limited functionality (specific task only)
- Uses Separation partifunct

**Partivant (in [[Distributed-Organisms-Overview|Distributed Organisms]]):**
- Permanently separated body in multi-body organism
- NOT viable independently
- Never rejoins (never was joined)
- Specialized function within DO

**Critical Difference:**
- Exovant = detachable tool
- Partivant = permanent distributed body

---

## Distinction from Other Strategies

### [[DO-vs-SB-vs-JB-Comparison|SB vs DO vs JB]]

**Separated Behaviors (SB):**
- One body temporarily separates part (exovant)
- Exovant not viable independently
- Must rejoin parent
- Examples: Throwjaw, Drophunter Blimp

**[[Distributed-Organisms-Overview|Distributed Organisms (DO)]]:**
- Multiple bodies (partivants) permanently separated
- None viable independently
- Never joined in first place
- Examples: DO-ScrubDeer, rDO-Duskmantle

**[[Combinant-Behaviors|Combinant Behaviors (JB)]]:**
- Multiple independent organisms join temporarily
- Each viable independently
- Temporary fusion, then separate
- Examples: Stringbirds, Ribbonfish

---

## Why Separated Behaviors Evolve

### Extended Reach Without Full Body

**Advantages:**
- **Task-specific deployment:** Send only what's needed
- **Risk minimization:** Main body remains safe
- **Extended range:** Reach beyond normal body limits
- **Energy efficiency:** Deploy small part vs. moving whole body

**Use Cases:**
- **Hunting:** Deploy attack structure ([[46c-Drophunter-Blimp|Drophunter hunter exovant]])
- **Defense:** Deploy weapon ([[46b-Tossclaw|Tossclaw]])
- **Feeding:** Deploy capture structure ([[46a-Throwjaw|Throwjaw]])
- **Exploration:** Deploy sensor package (rare, theoretical)

## Types of Exovants

### By Complexity

**Low-Function Exovants:**
- Simple, pre-programmed actions
- Minimal [[33-Brain-Partifuncts|nervous system]] (reflexes only)
- Short deployment time (seconds to minutes)
- Examples: [[46a-Throwjaw|Throwjaw]], [[46b-Tossclaw|Tossclaw]]

**High-Function Exovants:**
- Complex, autonomous behavior
- Significant neural tissue (decision-making)
- Longer deployment time (minutes to hours)
- Example: [[46c-Drophunter-Blimp|Drophunter Blimp]] hunter exovant

## Exovant Structure

### What Gets Separated

**Physical Composition:**
- Multiple [[Partifunct Core Definition|partifuncts]] (like any body part)
- [[Skeletal-Systems|Skeletal support]]
- [[Circulatory-Systems|Blood supply]] (limited, temporary reserves)
- [[Respiratory-Systems|Respiratory capacity]] (minimal, short-term only)
- [[33-Brain-Partifuncts|Neural control]] (varies by type)
- Muscles and connective tissue

**What's Missing:**
- Complete [[Digestive-Systems|digestive system]] (cannot sustain itself)
- [[3B-Womb-Partifunct-Systems|Womb]] (cannot reproduce)
- Full circulatory loops (dependent on parent)
- Most critical organ systems
- Long-term metabolic capability

**Result:**
Functional for specific task, but **countdown to death** begins at separation.

## Separation Mechanism

### How Detachment Occurs

**Anatomical Design:**
- Pre-formed separation plane (weak point in skeleton/tissue)
- Muscular control (deliberate separation, not accidental)
- Specialized joints (ball-and-socket designed for release)
- Vascular sealing (rapid clotting at separation point)

**Separation Process:**
1. Organism decides to deploy exovant
2. Hormonal signal triggers preparation
3. Blood flow to exovant reduced (gradual)
4. Muscular contraction releases joint/connection
5. Exovant separates (physical detachment)
6. Rapid sealing at separation point (both sides)
7. Exovant begins independent action

**Energy Reserve:**
- Exovant carries stored energy (glycogen analog)
- Limited oxygen supply
- Functional for minutes to hours (depends on activity level)
- Depletion = death if not rejoined

## Exovant Behavior

### Pre-Programmed Actions

**Low-Function:**
- Ballistic trajectory (Throwjaw)
- Simple grab-and-return (Tossclaw)
- Reflex responses only
- No learning or adaptation
- [[33a-Motor-Control|MOTO brain]] fragment only

**High-Function:**
- Autonomous hunting (Drophunter hunter exovant)
- Decision-making (target selection)
- Complex maneuvers (pursuit, capture)
- Multiple [[33-Brain-Partifuncts|brain partifuncts]] (MOTO, SENS, THRT)
- Limited learning during deployment

---

## Rejoining Mechanism

### Return to Parent

**Navigation:**
- Chemical tracking (pheromone trail from parent)
- Visual recognition (parent body features)
- Auditory signals (parent calls)
- Radio signals (some species, rare)

**Reattachment:**
1. Exovant returns to parent body
2. Locates separation point (chemical markers)
3. Aligns correctly (physical guides)
4. Connection structures engage (reverse of separation)
5. Vascular reconnection (blood flow resumes)
6. Neural reintegration (nerve bundles reconnect)
7. Tissue healing begins (hours to days)

**Time Limit:**
- Low-function exovants: Minutes (5-30 typical)
- High-function exovants: Hours (1-4 typical)
- Beyond time limit: Exovant dies, cannot rejoin
- Parent can regenerate lost exovant (weeks to months)

## Evolutionary Perspective

### Why This Strategy Succeeds

**Advantages:**
- Extended effective body range
- Minimize parent body risk
- Task-specific optimization
- Energy efficiency (deploy only what's needed)

**Disadvantages:**
- Risk of exovant loss (predation, failure to return)
- Energy cost of separation/rejoining
- Regeneration cost if lost
- Limited exovant lifespan (time pressure)

**Selection Pressures:**
- Environments where full-body approach too risky
- Prey that requires specialized capture method
- Predators that target large bodies
- [[Natural-Selection-on-TPIV|Multi-level selection]] on exovant success rate

---

## Metabolic Constraints

### The Clock is Ticking

**Energy Depletion:**
- Exovant carries limited fuel
- Active tissue burns through reserves quickly
- Brain tissue most demanding
- Movement costly

**Oxygen Limitation:**
- Small oxygen reserves (no functional lungs usually)
- Anaerobic metabolism possible (short-term)
- Lactic acid analog buildup
- Fatigue and failure inevitable

**Waste Buildup:**
- No excretory function
- Metabolic waste accumulates
- Toxic at high concentrations
- Another countdown to failure

**Result:**
Exovant must complete task and rejoin **before** metabolic collapse.

---

## Failure Modes

### What Goes Wrong

**Navigation Failure:**
- Lost exovant cannot find parent
- Wanders until energy depletion
- Dies within hours

**Predation:**
- Exovant attacked and killed/eaten
- Parent must regenerate
- Significant energy cost

**Injury:**
- Damaged exovant cannot rejoin properly
- Parent may reject (infection risk)
- Exovant dies, regeneration required

**Time Limit Exceeded:**
- Exovant depletes reserves before return
- Dies en route to parent
- Parent must regenerate

**Regeneration:**
- Parent can regenerate lost exovant
- Costly process (weeks to months)
- Vulnerable during regeneration (reduced capability)
- [[3B-Womb-Partifunct-Systems|Womb-like growth]] process

## Comparative Notes

### Unique to TPIV

**Earth Analogs:**
- **Lizard tail autotomy:** Tail drops, distracts predator, but doesn't rejoin
- **Octopus arm autonomy:** Arm continues moving after severance, but doesn't rejoin
- **Starfish arm regeneration:** Lost arm regrows, but doesn't reattach

**TPIV Unique Aspects:**
- Exovant **performs useful task** (not just distraction)
- Exovant **returns and reintegrates** (functional rejoining)
- Exovant has **complex behavior** (not just reflex)
- [[Non-Homochirality|Modular biology]] enables this strategy

## Research Observations

### Field Studies

**Frequency:**
- Separated behaviors less common than [[Distributed-Organisms-Overview|DOs]] or [[Combinant-Behaviors|JB]]
- ~20 documented species
- Most are ambush predators or aerial hunters
- Rare in herbivores (no clear advantage)

**Success Rates:**
- Low-function exovants: 85-95% rejoin successfully
- High-function exovants: 60-75% rejoin successfully
- Loss primarily due to predation
- Navigation failure rare (chemical tracking reliable)
