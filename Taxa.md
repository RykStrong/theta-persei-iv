# Taxa (TPIV-Specific)

**Definition:** A taxon on TPIV refers to a statistical assemblage: a common configuration of partifuncts that occurs frequently enough to produce a recognizable body plan or ecological role  
**Not a Genetic Species:** It is a repeatable phenotype—a recurring composite structure that emerges from shared environmental and developmental conditions  
**Key Insight:** There is no single partifunct or genome that defines composite forms

---

## What Is a Taxon?

### Statistical Assemblage

A **taxon** on TPIV refers to a **statistical assemblage**: a common configuration of partifuncts that occurs frequently enough to produce a recognizable body plan or ecological role.

**It is not a genetic species.** It is a **repeatable phenotype**—a recurring composite structure that emerges from shared environmental and developmental conditions.

**Examples:**
- [[TPM-Zebra-Grazer|Zebra Grazer]] family: Grazing organisms on the steppe
- [[83-LBB-Little-Brown-Birds|LBB Feeders]]: "Little brown bird" catch-all category
- [[Drophunter-Blimps|Drophunter Blimp]]: Separated behavior apex hunter

---

## Why Taxa Replace Species

### No Species on TPIV

What we call species do not exist on TPIV. What we have is a continuum of life forms that reach across a range of environments.

**Example:**
With grazing animals, on Earth there are deer, sheep, cattle, and camels (among others), while on TPIV there are a range of creatures from the size of a large dog through the size of a large bison that share different manifestations of the same partifuncts.

Looking at a prairie, one could see small grazers like antelope, and large ones like elephants, but also creatures of every step and stage between the two forms.

### No Central Genome

There is no single partifunct that defines the composite forms of higher animals. Their form is determined by the selection pressures of the environment, acting on the partifuncts that make up their parts.

## Quantifying Taxa

### PSV and MDI Metrics

We quantify these groupings using:

**[[27b-Partifunct-Set-Variance|Partifunct Set Variance (PSV)]]:** How consistent is the set of modules across individuals?

**[[27c-Morphological-Diversity-Index|Morphological Divergence Index (MDI)]]:** How different do those individuals look, despite sharing modules?

---

### Examples by Metrics

**High PSV, Moderate MDI:**
- **Zebra Grazer** (PSV 83.2%, MDI 4.6)
- Consistent partifunct set
- Look somewhat alike
- Share many partifuncts

**Low PSV, High MDI:**
- **LBB Feeders** (PSV 23%, MDI 8.2)
- Near-chaotic diversity
- Built from common parts
- Highly variable appearance

## Naming Conventions

### Functional Classification

We use functional and visual descriptors to make up the common name of the composite organism.

**Functional names use:**
- Energy source
- Mode of locomotion (or anchoring)
- Bodily function or structure
- Size of the life form

**Convention:**
The convention for common names is to be two words, with one word being descriptive and one word being functional. There are no absolute rules.

**Examples:**
- "Zebra Grazer": Pattern + ecological role
- "Drophunter Blimp": Behavior + form
- "Crimson Strider": Color + locomotion

---

## Variability Within Taxa

### Offspring Variability

What is possible to assemble and exactly how it is assembled can vary greatly, so a herd of grazers (for example) do not appear uniform in the way a herd of antelope do.

**Example - Grazer Herd:**
- Some grazers will have more legs, some fewer
- Some will have two mouths
- Some will have additional genomic forms as well as the basic three
- Some individuals will have additional appendages for perception, defense, signaling, etc.

**All recognizably "Zebra Grazer" despite variation**

## Visualizing Taxa

### Partifunct Expression Graph (PEG)

To visualize these statistics, xenobiologists plot the observed organisms as multidimensional points on a **Partifunct Expression Graph (PEG)**:

- Each dimension corresponds to a specific measurable trait or the quantitative presence/expression level of certain partifuncts
- The resulting point cloud illustrates visually the shape of a taxon:
  - **Tight Cluster**: Low MDI, high PSV
  - **Diffuse Cloud**: High MDI, lower PSV

Scientists then quantify this shape numerically (PSV%, MDI value), allowing objective comparison between groups.

---

## Example Research Note

**Typical PEG Note:**
*"Composite 'Zebra Grazer' forms tight clusters (PSV=87%) but remains morphologically diverse (MDI=4.8), particularly in limb number and joint types."*

**Field Observation:**
*"The population sampled near the Olan River crossing exhibited a PSV of 92% (indicating high partifunct consistency) but had an elevated MDI of 5.4. The river-crossing behavior appears to encourage wider morphological expression—approximately 35% more variable than average populations, likely reflecting adaptive pressures."*

---

## Practical Communication

### Succinct Statements

Field researchers typically give succinct statements summarizing these statistical descriptors, like:

*"Observed group identified as 'River Guardian' variant, PSV 76%, MDI 6.1—indicating significant morphological and functional variability."*

These statistics convey a clear snapshot of the composite organisms' consistency and variability, efficiently substituting traditional biological definitions of "species."

## How Taxa Are Used

### Taxon Partifunct Manifest (TPM)

We have used the term **taxon** to identify the commonly occurring partifunct assemblages, rather than 'species'.

For documentation, see [[TPM-Template|Taxon Partifunct Manifest]] format—a structured document describing:
- Observed phenotype
- Partifunct architecture
- Statistical uniformity (PSV)
- Morphological diversity (MDI)
- Modularity signature
